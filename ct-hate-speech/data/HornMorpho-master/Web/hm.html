Morphological Processing for Languages of the Horn of Africa

What is HornMorpho?

HornMorpho is a program written in the Python programming language that does morphological analysis and generation of words in various languages of the Horn of Africa.

Who is it for?

To use HornMorpho, you should

  1. be at least somewhat familiar with Python programming
  2. know at least one of the languages covered by the program, and
  3. know some basic morphological terms and concepts, for example, root, suffix, tense, and person

What can you do with it?

The purpose of HornMorpho is to analyze or generate morphologically complex words in one of the languages covered. Morphologically complex words are those consisting of multiple morphemes, that is, a root or stem and one or more prefixes, suffixes, or "templates" (more about these later). For example, the Amharic noun ለጓደኞቿ lǝgʷaddǝññoččʷa 'to her friends', consists of the stem ጓደኛ gʷaddǝñña, the suffixes ኦች -očč 'plural' and ዋ -wa 'her', and the prepositional prefix (or case marker) ለ lǝ- 'to'.

The most complex words in all of these languages are verbs, which indicate tense-aspect-modality ("past", "continuous", "imperative", etc.), subject agreement ("1st person singular", "2nd person feminine plural", etc.), derivational categories ("passive", "causative", etc.), and a number of other features, depending on the language. Verbs are the main focus of HornMorpho.

Other words — prepositions, postpositions, conjunctions, adverbs — are mostly ignored by HornMorpho since they either have no internal structure or consist of fixed forms that are treated as lexical items (words) rather than as analyzable forms. For example, the Amharic adverb በተጨማ bǝtǝč'ǝmmari 'in addition' is derived from the verb root <č'mr> 'add', but it appears in this form so often that an analysis of the word is not particularly helpful, so it is treated as an unanalyzed word by the program.

Morphological analysis takes a word and either finds the root or stem and a set of grammatical features or segments the word into its constituent morphemes. I will refer to the first of these as "grammatical analysis" and the second as "segmentation" For example, grammatical analysis applied to the Amharic noun ለጓደኞቿ yields stem=ጓደኛ, poss=3sf, num=plur, prep=ለ. That is, this noun has the stem ጓደኛ ('friend'), plural number, a 3rd person singular feminine possessive, and the preposition (or case marker) ለ. "Stemming" or "lemmatization" is a subset of the process of grammatical analysis; it returns only the stem, root, or lemma (a canonical form of the stem or root) of the word, in this case ጓደኛ gʷaddǝñña. Segmentation applied to the same word yields ለ-<ጓደኛ>-ኦች-ዋ (or lǝ-<gʷaddǝñña>-očč-wa).

Morphological generation is the reverse process. It starts with a root or stem and a set of grammatical features and outputs a word form. For example, given stem=ጓደኛ, poss=3sf, num=plur, prep=ለ, generation yields the word ለጓደኞቿ.

Why would you want a program that does morphological analysis or generation? Languages with complex morphology suffer from the problem of data scarcity. Such languages have many more possible words than a language such as English or French, and most of the possible words have little likelihood of occurring in a given corpus or of occurring so few times that it is impossible to generalize about them. If you are training a machine translation system, a speech recognition system, or a parser in such a language, it may be possible to alleviate the data sparsity problem by replacing words with grammatical analyses, stems, or lemmas.
